# Guided Reading Schema

Use this file for Board 1: Original Guided Reading.

The core rule is simple: stay aligned to the paper's reading order.
Do not jump around the paper just because one paragraph feels more interesting than another.

## Unit of annotation

Work at the level of:

- one paragraph
- one displayed formula block
- one figure or one tightly related figure group
- one table when the table carries real evidence

Do not skip several dense paragraphs and cover them with one vague note.

## Two scales of explanation

Each section needs two layers.

### Section thread note

At the start of every major section, explain:

1. what question this section is trying to answer
2. why the paper needs this section at this point
3. how it connects to the previous section
4. what the reader should watch for in the upcoming paragraphs

### Local paragraph note

For each paragraph, explain these slots in plain language:

1. **Local role**
   What is this paragraph doing: setup, definition, contrast, derivation step, design choice, result, or conclusion?

2. **Link from the previous part**
   Is it continuing, narrowing, turning, justifying, contrasting, or closing?

3. **Core information**
   Pull out the one claim, variable, assumption, design choice, or result that matters most here.

4. **What the reader may miss**
   Point out hidden assumptions, shorthand, missing justification, or scope limits when material.

5. **What to remember before moving on**
   Give the shortest useful carry-forward idea for the next paragraph.

## Results paragraphs

When the paragraph reports an experiment or comparison, make sure the note answers:

- what is being compared
- what metric is changing
- which direction is better
- why this result matters to the paper's thesis
- whether the gain looks meaningful or marginal

## Method paragraphs

When the paragraph describes a method step, make sure the note answers:

- what input this step takes
- what output it produces
- why this step exists
- what would break if this step were removed
- whether the paper assumes something idealized here

## Literature review paragraphs

Do not only list names.
Explain what role the cited work plays:

- background
- competing route
- technical ancestor
- missing baseline
- target of critique

## Style

Keep the note readable by a student who is not yet fluent in the paper's dialect.
Do not translate every sentence into jargon again.
Explain what the paragraph is doing in the paper's argument.

The page should feel like someone is helping the reader walk through the paper, not like someone is decorating OCR output.
